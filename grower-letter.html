A Letter to New York's Licensed Growers

Licensed growers of New York,

When weed prices fall, buyers don't line up to tell you. It's just quiet. The phone doesn't ring. Another leg down.

It took us three and a half years for the falling prices in California to drop below our marginal cost of production. Along the path we watched as the tempest devastated friends and neighbors as those of us remaining burned more of our furniture to stay warm and fend off aphids, spider-mites and HLVd.

You are on a big call and someone points out that waiting the storm out might be easier to endure without any plants in the ground because each one of them is losing money. It's a shitty day when the spreadsheet tells you to lay everyone off. Didn't matter, another leg down.

As dominoes fell, we were afforded some protection resulting from our larger scale but closures swept down through the state from the north, loosely correlated to hours of sunlight and winter temps. In Monterey we outlasted most of the triangle but ultimately our greenhouses couldn't compete with the bountiful sun of Santa Barbara only 200 miles to our south.

I run Lowell Herb Company and I have a profound respect for what you do. Like many of you, I have witnessed the apocalypse caused by uncapped licensing. We must do better in New York.

This table is the reason I'm writing you today. In California, supply outraced demand by a wide margin and never caught up. Here in New York, demand is growing 30% faster than production capacity. Retail sales have tripled while cultivation has only doubled. Per-store revenue is up 30%, not down.

New York is doing something different. The market is young, the licensing is tiered, and the trajectory is healthy — but only if we defend it together. An uncapped free-for-all would turn this table upside down in a year. We've seen that movie. It ends badly.

I'm telling you this because of something we keep hearing from growers around the state. There's a recurring theme: more and more canopy is being directed toward genetics engineered to perform as fresh frozen — flower that goes straight to the freezer instead of the drying rack, destined for live resin, live rosin, and infused prerolls. On its face it makes sense: less drying infrastructure, faster turnaround, and the concentrate side of the market is where the excitement is.

But look at the second table. The concentrate market — the entire category that absorbs fresh frozen biomass — is 11% of total sales. Flower and prerolls together are 45%.

That raises a question that none of us can answer alone: how many growers are actually making this pivot, and can the market handle it? If 5% of the state's canopy shifts to fresh frozen, that's one thing. If 30% does, we are likely to see a glut of frozen weed without a home. The concentrate market is a teacup. A coordinated rush in any direction is the kind of supply glut nobody wants to discover they're part of until it's too late.

That's exactly why we're doing this survey — and why your participation matters. The only way to know whether you're heading toward an opportunity or into a crowded room is to see what everybody else is doing. Right now, nobody has that picture.

Here are the questions the survey is designed to answer — for all of us, together:

• How many plants are being flowered this season vs. last?
Is the market expanding or contracting at the plant level?

• How many unique genetics are being run this season vs. last?
Are growers narrowing to washer-specific genetics or diversifying?

• What share of harvest is going to fresh frozen vs. dried flower?
Is the fresh frozen pivot real — and how fast is it happening?

• Is fresh frozen biomass selling as bulk or moving into CPG?
See who's capturing the margin vs. selling raw material.

• What's the split between flower sold as CPG vs. bulk wholesale?
Benchmark your route to market against the rest of the state.

• What's the single biggest challenge facing your operation?
Find out if your biggest problem is everybody's biggest problem.

We are inviting every licensed cultivator in New York to participate. Every respondent gets access to the full aggregated results — so you can see the answers to every question above, in real time, as responses come in. Your individual responses are private. Only the aggregate gets published. It takes about 5 minutes.

This isn't a Lowell survey. It's an industry survey. We're just hosting it.

Some folks who buy flower on the bulk market want their retail customers to believe they grew the flower themselves. In contrast, we prefer to share your stories with as many people as we can so they too can be proud of our cannabis community.
